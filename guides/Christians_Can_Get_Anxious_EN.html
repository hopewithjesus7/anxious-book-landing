Authors' Note

Authors' Note

"I pray, but the anxiety doesn't go away. Does that mean I have no faith?"

This single sentence is the one the three of us — across the counseling room, the clinic, and the church — have heard most often over several decades. Inside that question lie the pain of anxiety, the guilt of believing one's faith is lacking, and the confusion of not knowing whether or not to seek help. This book was written to answer exactly that question.

Christians can get anxious. This is not a failure of faith. Anxiety is a signal the body, mind, and soul that God created send when they sense a threat — the psalmist walked that road, so did the prophet Elijah, and so did the apostle Paul. The problem is not that we feel anxiety, but how we understand it and how we care for it.

This book is co-written by a pastor, a clinical psychologist, and a psychiatrist, all serving on the front lines. As far as we know, this is the first attempt in Korea to integrate biblical insight, psychological understanding, and psychiatric knowledge within a single flow. Until now, books on anxiety have stayed within spiritual exhortation, stopped at psychological explanation, or delivered only medical information — each on its own. But a human being is a whole in which soul, mind, and body are connected as one. To truly understand and heal anxiety, all three dimensions must be addressed together.

We hope this book becomes a warm hand extended without condemnation to those going through hard times with anxiety. Your anxiety is not proof that you are a bad Christian. On the contrary, even within that anxiety, God is at work.

Prologue — To You Who Are Anxious Even at Church

To You Who Are Anxious Even at Church

One Sunday morning, Minsu, sitting in the choir loft, suddenly felt as if he couldn't breathe. His heart raced, his palms sweated, and he grew dizzy. Even as he kept singing, his mind was in turmoil. 'Why am I like this? What's wrong with me? Could God be punishing me?'

Minsu had served the church as a deacon for over ten years. He led Bible studies, mentored the young adults, and was a faithful provider at home. But worries were endless — work, his children's education, health, finances. Night after night he couldn't sleep easily, and his shoulders and neck were stiff as stone. Hearing his situation, his district leader counseled him with concern: "Cast it all on God in faith."

Minsu went even more diligently to the dawn prayer meetings. But the anxiety didn't lessen — only the self-blame for lacking faith grew. He finally went to the hospital, where a psychiatrist diagnosed generalized anxiety disorder and prescribed medication. With the medicine, his symptoms eased somewhat. But comments from those around him — "If you take psychiatric meds you'll get addicted," "You should overcome it by faith, not lean on drugs" — left him confused again. He also tried counseling. Learning about his distorted thought patterns helped, yet it felt somehow empty, because that work wasn't connected to his faith.

Minsu felt as though he were living in three separate worlds. At church they spoke of faith, at the hospital they told him about brain chemicals, and in the counseling room they told him to change his thoughts and behaviors. But he couldn't see how any of it connected into one.

An Integrated Approach Is Needed

Minsu's story is not unusual. There are more hurting believers in the church than we think. There are even reports that the rate of anxiety disorders among Christians is higher than in the general population. The bigger problem is that many Christians, like Minsu, receive only 'fragmented' help. Medical treatment, counseling, and spiritual care are received in pieces, never integrated.

But God created us as whole beings. Our body, mind, and spirit are deeply connected, so if only one part is addressed, true healing is hard to come by. Medicine teaches us that anxiety is related to chemical imbalances in the brain; psychology explains how past experiences, thought patterns, and learned behaviors produce our present anxiety; and Scripture teaches where the source of true peace and ultimate safety lies. All of these are gifts God has given.

An integrated approach does not mean forcibly stitching these three together. Rather, it means recognizing that they were one to begin with. Medicine reveals the workings of the body God created; psychology uncovers how the mind, made in God's image, operates; and Scripture leads us to true healing and maturity within a relationship with the God who created all things and rules over them even now.

The Journey of This Book

This book does not condemn anxiety as sin. Nor does it treat it merely as a 'spiritual problem that will be solved with more prayer.' Part One (chapters 1–3) begins by understanding anxiety. How does normal anxiety differ from anxiety that won't stop? What is actually happening inside our brain? And where does anxiety come from? We unpack these step by step. Part Two (chapters 4–6) turns to Scripture. Elijah was afraid; so was David. We look into the moments when people of faith despaired and burned out, and how God met them right there. Part Three (chapters 7–11) honestly addresses the places where believers most often stumble — medication and counseling, and change in thought and behavior. Part Four (chapters 12–15) returns to the place of the soul, finding together how to turn worry into prayer, how to abide before God, and how to enjoy joy and secure attachment.

Jesus says, "Come to me, all who labor and are heavy laden, and I will give you rest" (Matt. 11:28, ESV). This invitation is not given only to those whose faith is perfect. It is an invitation to the weary, the anxious, those broken down by self-blame, those discouraged because peace doesn't come even when they pray.

That day, in the church counseling room, Minsu told his story and wept for the first time. It was a cry that broke loose as he set down the burden he had carried alone for so long. That weeping was, for him, the first step toward healing. Your first step, too, is beginning now.

CHAPTER 01

Why Am I Anxious When I Believe So Earnestly?

Checking My Own Anxiety

Sujin and Miyeong are friends in the same church young-adult group. Both had an important certification exam coming up.

The evening before the exam, Sujin felt a little nervous, her chest fluttering slightly. She prayed, "God, please help me do well tomorrow," and went to bed thirty minutes earlier than usual. She tossed for a bit but soon fell asleep, and in the morning she encouraged herself — "I can do this, I prepared hard" — and headed to the exam.

Miyeong was completely different. From 7 p.m. the night before, anxiety set in. 'What if I fail? My parents will be disappointed. Everyone will see me as a failure.' The thoughts chained on endlessly. She tried to pray, but even then worries washed over her like waves. She barely fell asleep around 3 a.m., then woke again an hour later. In the end she went to the exam having hardly slept, her mind blank, unable to solve even problems she normally found easy. Even after the exam, Miyeong's anxiety ran on endlessly — from 'What if my results are bad?' to 'How will I pay tuition?' to 'Can I serve properly at church?'

Anxiety Can Both Help and Hurt Me

The two friends' stories raise an important question. Is anxiety itself the problem? The answer is "No." Anxiety is a gift God has given us — an alarm system that detects danger and protects us. The moderate tension we feel before an exam or an important presentation helps us prepare more thoroughly. In 2018, a Yale University neuroscience team published findings that people who experience a moderate level of anxiety have better problem-solving and risk-assessment abilities than those who feel no anxiety at all.

Sujin's anxiety was normal and healthy. It was a response to a real situation, its intensity was appropriate, it disappeared when the exam ended, and it helped her. But Miyeong's anxiety went beyond the protective mechanism meant to guard her and was harming her instead. Even after the situation ended it didn't stop but moved on to other worries; even praying and reassuring herself couldn't stop it; and above all it markedly lowered the quality of her daily life.

Anxiety That Guards the Heart, and Anxiety That Wears It Down

How can we distinguish normal anxiety from pathological anxiety? In psychiatry, pathological anxiety has four features.

  • Excessiveness — Miyeong's anxiety was too strong for the situation. She reacted as if her whole life hung on a single certification exam.
  • Persistence — Normal anxiety disappears when the danger is gone, but Miyeong's continued even after the exam and shifted to other worries.
  • Uncontrollability — Sujin could manage her anxiety through prayer, but Miyeong couldn't stop it even by praying and soothing herself.
  • Disruption of daily life — Miyeong couldn't sleep, her concentration dropped, she did poorly on the exam, and her fellowship with God was disrupted.

A simple self-check can help you examine your own anxiety. Answer the following honestly.

  • For most days over the past six months, I have worried excessively.
  • I find it hard to stop or control that worry on my own.
  • Worry keeps me from sleeping well, or I wake often.
  • Without any particular reason, I am always tense or easily tired.
  • Worry interferes with my work, study, relationships, or faith life.
  • I tend to connect even small matters to the worst-case scenario.

If three or more apply to you, your anxiety may have gone beyond the normal range. But the important thing is not to blame yourself. This does not mean you are weak or that your faith is lacking — it is simply a signal that you need help.

Understanding Generalized Anxiety Disorder

Miyeong eventually went to a counseling office, where she learned that her experience corresponded to "generalized anxiety disorder." Severe anxiety is not necessarily a disorder. Modern psychiatry checks two broad criteria: one is that the symptoms significantly reduce one's ability to function, and the other is that the person experiences great distress. A diagnosis of a disorder is made if even one of these — not necessarily both — applies.

Miyeong spent most of her day worrying. From the moment she opened her eyes — 'Can I get today's tasks done properly?' — until she fell asleep, the worries about work, family, health, finances, church service, and relationships never ceased. In a 2017 study of 500 patients with generalized anxiety disorder by a Harvard Medical School team, 78% reported "having worried a lot since childhood." The harder they tried not to worry, the more they worried — a paradox. At work her mistakes increased and she received warnings, she found going to her church small group burdensome, and her marriage grew distant.

What Happens in the Brain When We're Anxious

"Why does my brain keep worrying like this? I want to stop, but I can't." Miyeong's question is very important. When we understand how our brain creates and regulates anxiety, we blame ourselves less and treatment becomes more effective.

First, we need to understand the amygdala. The amygdala is a small, almond-shaped structure deep in the brain, and its main role is detecting danger. All the information we see, hear, and feel passes through it as it rapidly judges, 'Is this dangerous?' But in a person with generalized anxiety disorder, this 'fire alarm' is set too sensitively, so it overreacts even in situations that aren't actually dangerous.

Fortunately, our brain has a system that regulates the amygdala's overreaction: the prefrontal cortex. Located right behind the forehead, it handles logical thinking, planning, decision-making, and emotional regulation. When the amygdala sounds the alarm — "Danger!" — the prefrontal cortex says, "Wait, let's check again whether it's really dangerous." But in people with generalized anxiety disorder, this regulating function of the prefrontal cortex is weakened. Miyeong said, "There are two voices in my head. One shouts that something terrible is happening; the other says it'll be okay. The first voice is much louder."

For the amygdala and the prefrontal cortex to communicate, they need messengers. Brain cells (neurons) are separated by tiny gaps (synapses), and what crosses those gaps to deliver the message is a neurotransmitter. There are three main neurotransmitters related to anxiety.

  • Serotonin — involved in mood, sleep, appetite, and anxiety regulation; it suppresses the amygdala's overreaction and helps the prefrontal cortex work well. The SSRI (selective serotonin reuptake inhibitor) Miyeong was prescribed keeps serotonin in the synapse longer, which makes it effective for generalized anxiety disorder.
  • GABA — the brain's "sedative," which inhibits the excitation of nerve cells. There are reports that GABA was deficient in the prefrontal cortex of patients with generalized anxiety disorder, which is related to the brain not being able to "brake" well.
  • Norepinephrine (noradrenaline) — puts us in a state of arousal and raises alertness. In moderate amounts it boosts concentration, but in excess it triggers anxiety and restlessness; in patients with generalized anxiety disorder this level is chronically high.

A healthy brain maintains balance among these systems. But in the brain of a person with generalized anxiety disorder, the amygdala is overactivated and the prefrontal cortex can't regulate it sufficiently, while serotonin and GABA are deficient and norepinephrine is too high. Such changes even alter the brain's structure, enlarging the amygdala and shrinking the prefrontal cortex. A 2021 study published in Nature found that these brain changes are both a "cause" and a "result" of generalized anxiety disorder.

A Brain That Finds Its Balance Again

There is good news. With appropriate treatment, the brain can be substantially restored. The brain is astonishingly flexible, adapting and changing throughout life — this is called neuroplasticity. Through medication, exercise, psychotherapy, and spiritual training, we can readjust the brain's chemistry, reconstruct its neural circuits, and learn new patterns of response. Even if you are older or have a mental disorder, the brain can still change.

After six months of integrated treatment, Miyeong said: "Now I see anxiety differently. I used to be displeased with my weak self and blame myself. But now I think, 'My amygdala is overreacting; I need to activate my prefrontal cortex.'" When we understand anxiety well, we can handle it effectively. Stepping out of the pattern of self-blame and despair, we can hold on to hope and treat ourselves gently.

CHAPTER 02

My Chest Feels Tight and I'm Full of Worry

Looking Closely at the Symptoms of Generalized Anxiety Disorder

Jeonghun is a mid-career office worker in his forties and a church deacon. When he visited his pastor, who serves as a counselor, he said, "Pastor, I went to the hospital because my body hurt, not because of a psychiatric problem." For a year Jeonghun had made the rounds of various hospitals. His chest felt tight and he couldn't breathe, so thinking something was wrong with his heart he went to internal medicine — but every test came back normal, and all he heard was "Reduce your stress." His headaches were so bad he even had an MRI at neurology, but his brain was healthy. At orthopedics he received physical therapy for neck and shoulder pain, and at the dentist he was fitted with a mouthguard because he had worn down his teeth from grinding. To the dejected Jeonghun, the pastor asked, "Have you had a lot of worries lately?" Jeonghun's eyes widened. "How did you know? I always have so many worries and my head is so complicated. Does that have something to do with my body hurting?"

Anxiety Sends Signals Through the Body

We often think of body and mind as separate, but they aren't. God created us as integrated beings, and our thoughts and emotions affect the body immediately. According to a 2019 report by the American Psychological Association (APA), 95% of patients with generalized anxiety disorder experience at least one physical symptom, and 60% of them first go to the hospital believing it's a physical illness rather than a psychiatric problem.

Why does anxiety cause physical symptoms? It's because of our body's "fight-or-flight response." If you suddenly meet a bear in the forest, the brain immediately releases stress hormones like adrenaline and cortisol. Then the heart beats faster, breathing becomes rapid, muscles tense, digestion stops, and sweat flows. This is a perfect response for short-term survival. The problem is that the body of a person with generalized anxiety disorder reacts as if there were an attacking beast even when there isn't — perceiving everyday situations like a work presentation, a child's report card, or a health-checkup result as if they were life-threatening. This 'emergency mode' can even continue for months or years. It's like running a car engine at maximum RPM continuously — the body inevitably suffers.

Muscle Tension

"When I wake up in the morning my neck is stiff as a rock. It's as if I carried a heavy load on my shoulders all night." What tormented Jeonghun most was neck and shoulder pain. This is the most common physical symptom in patients with generalized anxiety disorder; according to a 2018 study at the University of Munich in Germany, 89% of patients experience chronic muscle tension, concentrated especially in the neck, shoulders, and jaw. The threats modern people face are not beasts but work stress, financial worries, and relational conflict, against which we can't physically fight or flee. With no outlet to release the tension, it becomes chronic, and we even tense our muscles in our sleep, so we wake up tired.

Sleep Disorders

Sleep disorders are a common and painful symptom in patients with generalized anxiety disorder. According to a meta-analysis published in the Sleep Medicine Journal in 2021, 70–90% of patients experience sleep problems. There are three types.

  • Sleep-onset difficulty — trouble falling asleep, the most common. When you lie down, the worries suppressed all day rush in at once.
  • Sleep-maintenance difficulty — waking frequently, and once awake it takes a long time to fall back asleep.
  • Non-restorative sleep — not feeling refreshed even after sleeping, because only shallow sleep is repeated without deep sleep.

A 2019 study at UC Berkeley found that just one night of sleep deprivation increases the next day's anxiety level by 30%. A vicious cycle forms: anxiety brings sleep disorders, sleep deprivation raises anxiety, and that anxiety produces even worse sleep deprivation.

Chronic Fatigue

Some people say they are always tired even though they've done nothing. Chronic fatigue is easy to overlook but a very common symptom. There are reasons. First, constantly tensed muscles consume energy, like holding a heavy object the whole time. Second, the chronic secretion of stress hormones drains the body. A 2019 Yale Medical School study found that people in a chronic state of anxiety burn on average 20% more calories per day than ordinary people. Third, the worrying itself consumes energy as the brain works without rest, constantly worrying and analyzing. Fourth, the quality of sleep is poor, so the cell repair and immune strengthening that should occur during deep sleep don't happen properly.

Other Physical Symptoms

The physical symptoms of generalized anxiety disorder affect nearly every system of the body. Like Jeonghun, many complain of cardiovascular symptoms — heart palpitations, chest pain, shortness of breath. A 2020 study in a journal of the American College of Cardiology found that chronically elevated heart rate and blood pressure actually strain the heart, raising the risk of cardiovascular disease. There is also the enteric nervous system, called the "second brain"; when the brain sends an anxiety signal, the gut reacts immediately, causing abdominal pain, nausea, diarrhea, or constipation. Headaches are common too — tension headaches arising from chronic tension in the neck, shoulders, and jaw; a 2019 headache-society study reported that 60% of patients experience headaches three or more times a week. Rapid, shallow breathing (hyperventilation) upsets the balance of oxygen and carbon dioxide in the blood, causing dizziness. A 2017 immunology-journal study found that chronic stress and anxiety lower immune function by 30–40%, with cortisol in particular suppressing the activity of immune cells.

Excessive Worry and Anxiety About the Future

Physical symptoms are painful, but the psychological symptoms are even harder. The core psychological symptom of generalized anxiety disorder is "excessive, uncontrollable worry." Patients worry more than ten times as much, with more than double the intensity, of ordinary people, and they worry about several kinds of things at once. A distinctive feature is that the worry is "chained": 'My throat hurts' leads to 'Did I catch a cold?' to 'What if it develops into pneumonia?' to 'If I'm hospitalized, who'll cover work?' to 'If I lose my job, how will I pay rent?' until it leaps to 'What if I become homeless?' Their speech is full of "what if" — "What if I make a mistake?", "What if they hate me?" Their mind is fixed not on the present but on a negative future. A 2020 University of Oxford study observed that when patients imagine a negative future event, the brain reacts in almost the same way as if actually experiencing it. An imagined catastrophe in the future creates real suffering in the present.

Cognitive Distortions

People with generalized anxiety disorder see the world through an 'anxiety lens.' In cognitive behavioral therapy this is called 'cognitive distortion.' Here are five common ones.

  • Catastrophizing — magnifying a small, trivial problem into the worst possible disaster.
  • Overgeneralization — taking one or two negative experiences as an eternal pattern.
  • All-or-nothing thinking — leaning to one of two extremes, with no gray zone.
  • Negative filtering — ignoring the positive and selectively focusing only on the negative.
  • Mind reading — being certain, without evidence, that others think negatively of you.

Cognitive distortion increases anxiety, and the increased anxiety in turn reinforces the distortion — a repeating vicious cycle.

Blurred Concentration and Difficulty Deciding

"Pastor, when I read the Bible I have to read the same verse five times. Even then I don't know what it says." This is Jeonghun's confession. About 90% of patients with generalized anxiety disorder have problems with attention and concentration, because worry eats up the brain's 'working memory.' Decision-making becomes hard too. Endlessly analyzing the negative outcomes of a choice, they get exhausted even choosing a menu at a restaurant — 'This one's too high in calories, that one's too expensive, and that one might upset my stomach...'

Faith Begins to Shake

"The Bible says 'do not be anxious,' but I worry every day. Even when I pray, the anxiety doesn't go away, and I can't concentrate during worship." The place generalized anxiety disorder wounds most deeply is the spiritual realm. More painful than a hurting body and a struggling mind is a shaken relationship with God. In a survey of 6,700 people in Korea Church Trends 2025 (Kyujang), more than one in five churchgoing Christians reported suffering from depression (23%) or anxiety (22%) within the previous two weeks. When anxiety is high, worry fills the mental space so there's no room to concentrate in prayer; reduced concentration makes the Bible hard to understand; and because of cognitive distortion, it's easy to receive a peripheral verse as mere law rather than grasping God's promises of love and care. Even during worship they worry — 'Am I praising properly right now? Am I the only one who feels no emotion?'

A Mind That Misunderstands God

Generalized anxiety disorder also distorts the way we perceive God. In a 2019 study in the psychology of religion at Baylor University, 63% of patients perceived God as authoritarian and punishing (a judging, strict, demanding God) and 47% as distant and indifferent, while only 28% perceived God as loving and caring. We often project our inner fear and anxiety onto God. Those who are harsh with themselves feel that God is harsh too, and those who cannot accept themselves feel that God does not accept them either. The confession "Jesus said he gives peace, yet I'm anxious and afraid every day — isn't that proof my faith is fake?" is the most painful spiritual struggle Christians with generalized anxiety disorder face.

Difficulties in Daily Life and Relationships

Generalized anxiety disorder erodes a person's whole life. During a presentation the mind goes blank; out of perfectionistic compulsion they keep revising a report and miss the deadline. Students may experience 'test anxiety disorder' — panic-level anxiety at every exam. Medical fees and medication costs, treatment for physical symptoms, losses from absences, and forfeited opportunity costs all add up to a heavy financial burden. Family relationships are damaged too. They get angry without particular reason, or constantly demand reassurance — "I didn't make a mistake, did I?" — wearing out their spouse. A parent's anxiety is internalized by children as the message that 'the world is a dangerous place.' Sadly, though patients desire social connection, they avoid it for fear of its risks, and even within the church community they find small groups burdensome 'for fear of being judged as weak in faith.'

The Light That Turns On Again

In this chapter we looked at how broadly and deeply generalized anxiety disorder affects life. But we must remember an important truth: "Understanding your symptoms is the first step toward healing." Many patients suffer for years without understanding their symptoms, thinking 'I'm the only strange one' and struggling alone. But everything we examined above is a typical symptom of generalized anxiety disorder — and it is not your fault. "After getting the diagnosis I actually felt relieved, because I learned it wasn't that I was weak — this is a medical condition that can be treated." That is Jeonghun's word. He finally began to blame himself less and to receive help. Generalized anxiety disorder is treatable.

CHAPTER 03

Where Did It All Begin?

Finding the Causes of Generalized Anxiety Disorder

"Why on earth am I so anxious?" Minji, an office worker in her late thirties, asked with tears welling up. She had grown up in an ordinary home with no major trauma, and while her siblings raised in the same environment were fine, she wanted to know why she alone was like this. "Why me?" is the question almost every patient with generalized anxiety disorder asks. They want to find a cause, and to blame someone — themselves, their parents, their environment, sometimes God. But the cause is not simple. Like several strands twisted into a rope, many factors are tangled together. This chapter looks at four main factors.

Inborn Temperament — Biological Factors

Some say, "It's not just me; everyone in our family worries a lot." The heritability of generalized anxiety disorder is about 30–40%, with the remaining 60–70% explained by psychological, social, and spiritual factors. According to 2019 data from Harvard Medical School and the U.S. CDC, the prevalence in the general population is about 5–6%, while the chance that an identical twin of a patient has the same disorder is about 32%, and a child's chance is 25%. It is not inevitable, but having a family member with the disorder somewhat raises the risk.

Inborn temperament also plays a role. About 20% of children are 'high-reactive' — easily frightened by new things or slow to adapt. According to a longitudinal study begun in 1980 by Harvard psychologist Jerome Kagan, about 40% of such children experienced an anxiety disorder in adolescence. But a 2018 follow-up showed that even high-reactive children developed resilience when parents raised them warmly and securely and exposed them gradually to new experiences, whereas overprotection — saying 'the world is a dangerous place' — made an anxiety disorder more likely. An imbalance in brain chemicals is also related. Chronic stress decreases serotonin and GABA and increases norepinephrine, and that change in turn produces more anxiety — a cyclical relationship. The good news is that this cycle also works in reverse: medication, psychotherapy, exercise, and spiritual training can all improve the imbalance.

Anxiety Grown from Experience — Psychological Factors

"I know there's a problem with my thinking, but I don't know how to change it." This is Minji's confession. Patients' distorted thought patterns are mostly learned, internalized through repeated experience and observation from childhood. Her mother's words — "Be careful so you don't get hurt!", "Prepare thoroughly so you don't make mistakes" — came from love, but Minji internalized the belief 'the world is dangerous, I must be careful, a mistake is a disaster.'

'Learned helplessness' also raises anxiety. When one learns that nothing one does can change the situation, the belief 'I am powerless' forms and gets applied to new situations too. Eunjeong, a teacher, said, "As a child I went through my father's alcoholism and violence. No matter how good I was, I couldn't stop it. Even now I always have a feeling that something bad is about to happen." A 2019 study in a journal of trauma psychology found that people who experienced learned helplessness in childhood are 2.8 times more likely to have generalized anxiety disorder as adults.

Avoidance tendencies also reinforce anxiety. Canceling an anxiety-provoking appointment brings temporary relief, which becomes negative reinforcement, leading to more and more avoidance. Going to a gathering but sitting in the corner without speaking, or giving a presentation but only reading from a script, can be hidden 'safety behaviors.' Eunjeong, anxious about speaking before her students, always stood behind the lectern; after counseling, when she came out front and walked among the students to teach, she was anxious at first but felt more comfortable after a few weeks.

Pressures That Crush Life — Social Factors

"I have no days off. Rent, employee wages, my children's school fees... the money going out is endless. I'm exhausted." Jeongsu, who is self-employed, was suffering from chronic stress. Short, intense acute stress isn't a big problem — our bodies are designed to handle it. The problem is continuous, endless chronic stress, which drains our reserves into a state of burnout; then the brain's stress-response system becomes hypersensitive and easily develops into generalized anxiety disorder.

Overwhelming traumatic experiences can also trigger anxiety disorders — abuse, neglect, witnessing accidents or disasters, exposure to violence, the sudden death of a loved one. Childhood trauma especially has lasting effects: trauma from ages 0–5 alters the brain's stress-response system as if fixing the thermostat at 'high risk,' so its influence lasts a long time. Junwoo said, "My father hit me even for small mistakes. Even now, the moment I stand before an authoritative person, my whole body freezes. My head knows 'this person won't hit me,' but my body sends danger signals."

Cultural background is also an invisible pressure. According to a 2021 World Health Organization (WHO) international comparison, the prevalence of anxiety disorders in East Asia is 8–10%, versus 4–6% in Northern Europe — a difference due to collectivism, an achievement-oriented and competitive atmosphere, and a culture of suppressed emotional expression and saving face. Specific life events — divorce, job loss, death, financial crisis, immigration — can be triggers; and interestingly, positive events such as marriage, a first childbirth, or a promotion also become stressors because they involve an uncertain future and require adaptation.

The Influence of an Unseen Dimension — Spiritual Factors

"Is my anxiety because of my sin?" Many ask this. First, let's be clear: having generalized anxiety disorder is not itself a sin. Just as having a physical illness is not a sin, having a mental-health condition is not itself a sin. In the Gospels, Jesus did not condemn the sick but invited them: "Come to me, all who labor and are heavy laden" (Matt. 11:28).

Yet sin and anxiety are related. Since humanity's fall, suffering, illness, and fear have entered the world, and generalized anxiety disorder is part of the reality of living in this fallen world. Sometimes a particular sin causes anxiety. Junho embezzled company funds for several years; he had nightmares every night and his heart pounded at the mere ring of a phone. In the end he confessed his sin and repaid the money; though he received legal punishment, his anxiety dropped dramatically and his heart was at ease. Conversely, anxiety can lead to sin. When anxiety is severe we may resent God, take our anger out on others, or use unhealthy coping such as drinking or overeating.

Sometimes anxiety arises from a distorted image of God. Studies of one's image of God generally present four types — the Authoritative God, the Critical God, the Benevolent God, and the Distant God — and those who hold the authoritarian or critical image feel more anxiety and psychological distress. Scripture says, "The LORD is merciful and gracious, slow to anger and abounding in steadfast love" (Ps. 103:8, ESV), yet those who grew up under strict teaching without sufficiently experiencing this character of God easily live in fear of a God who supervises and disciplines them all their lives.

Some also ask, "Could my anxiety be a satanic attack?" This is a sensitive subject requiring discernment. Some refuse treatment, viewing a mental-health problem as only a satanic attack, and receive only deliverance prayer until symptoms worsen; on the other hand, denying the reality of spiritual warfare and explaining it only as a biological or psychological problem is also incomplete. Scripture clearly says spiritual warfare exists: "For we do not wrestle against flesh and blood, but against... the spiritual forces of evil in the heavenly places" (Eph. 6:12, ESV). Balanced discernment, not leaning to either side, is needed. It is good to first receive a medical and psychological evaluation while also examining one's spiritual health. A spiritual attack is discerned by its fruit — a sudden, unexplained onset; no response at all to medical treatment; worsening especially during prayer; intense resistance to worship; making one hate God and others. But most patients with generalized anxiety disorder show none of these features; rather, they want to draw nearer to God but find it hard because of anxiety. One believer confessed, "At first I was sure it was a spiritual attack, so I fasted and prayed through the night, but nothing improved. Later I was diagnosed with generalized anxiety disorder at a psychiatric clinic, and while receiving medication and counseling I also continued prayer and spiritual training. The two worked together and I was healed. I learned that God works even through doctors and medicine."

Generalized anxiety disorder is sometimes accompanied by a deep crisis of faith. David confessed, "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?" (Ps. 22:1), and Elijah, after a stunning victory, prayed in deep depression and anxiety, "It is enough; now, O LORD, take away my life" (1 Kings 19:4). The cry of one passing through a crisis of faith is not sin but an honest expression of faith. Even faithful believers experience a dark night of the soul after great suffering or loss, but those who pass through it often become deeper, more mature people of faith. A senior deaconess who lost her son in a car accident confessed, "For two years I didn't know where God was. But looking back now, that wilderness time is what actually made my faith real. Before, I only 'knew about' God; now I have truly 'experienced' him. Even in the darkest place, God did not leave me."

It Can Be Restored Again

Biological, psychological, social, and spiritual factors do not act separately but are tangled together in complex ways. Eunjeong the teacher is an example. She inherited an anxious disposition from her mother, who had an anxiety disorder; she was temperamentally cautious and sensitive; she grew up in an unstable environment due to her father's business failures; and by imitating her mother's excessive worry she came to believe 'the world is dangerous.' In high school she experienced the trauma of a close friend's sudden death, and after becoming a teacher she faced the cultural pressure that 'one must not make mistakes,' the stresses of marriage, childbirth, and conflict with in-laws, post-childbirth thyroid decline and a vitamin-D deficiency, plus the distorted image of God that 'if I'm not perfect I won't be loved' — all of these tangled together and led to generalized anxiety disorder.

There are reasons we must understand this from multiple angles. First, we can stop blaming ourselves: Eunjeong's anxiety was the result of many factors, most of them beyond her control. Second, we can build an effective treatment plan: if the causes are varied, the treatment approaches must be varied too, and as the points where we can see improvement multiply, so does hope. The greatest message of hope is this: "Even if the causes cannot be perfectly removed, healing is still possible." Our brains are astonishingly plastic — able to form new neural pathways, learn new coping methods, and replace distorted beliefs with truth. Above all, we have a God who heals. He does not change our past, but he keeps that past from defining us, and he uses our wounds to shape us into people who are stronger, more compassionate, and dependent on God alone.

CHAPTER 04

Am I the Only One Who's Especially Weak?

The Fear That Even Elijah and Paul Experienced

Sujin is a leader in her church's young-adult group. She leads Bible study every week and is part of the worship team. Yet she couldn't understand why she, who believes in God, suffered so from anxiety. "Pastor, I think my faith is just too weak. I fall apart over nothing."

What about the people in the Bible? Were they free of all anxiety and fear, never shaken at all? Scripture often says "Do not be dismayed," "Do not be afraid," "Do not be anxious." That God's Word deals with anxiety and fear so often means that anxiety is one of the most basic and universal human emotions. This chapter looks at the moments people of faith experienced anxiety. God was always with them, and he is with you now too.

Elijah — Burnout After Victory

First Kings 18 is one of the most dramatic scenes. The prophet Elijah faced 850 prophets of Baal and Asherah on Mount Carmel. The false prophets cried out all day with no answer, but when Elijah prayed, the fire of the LORD fell and consumed the burnt offering, the wood, the stones, and the dust, and even licked up the water in the trench. The people fell on their faces and cried, "The LORD, he is God; the LORD, he is God" (1 Kings 18:39, ESV). It was an overwhelming victory.

But the story doesn't end there. In chapter 19, when Queen Jezebel threatened to kill him, Elijah fled, sat down under a broom tree in the wilderness, and prayed, "It is enough; now, O LORD, take away my life" (1 Kings 19:4, ESV). How could a man so bold before 850 prophets collapse like this? Modern psychology calls this 'burnout' or 'postvictory depression' — after a great achievement, energy is depleted, adrenaline drops, and one becomes emotionally vulnerable. It is like a church leader who finishes a revival meeting well, only to be hit by exhaustion and depression.

God did not rebuke Elijah with "Where is your faith?" His response was astonishingly gentle and tender.

  • He met physical needs — He sent an angel to feed Elijah bread and water. What looks like a spiritual problem sometimes comes from physical depletion. Sleep, food, and rest are the foundation of spiritual health.
  • He let him pour out his emotions — He asked, "What are you doing here, Elijah?" (1 Kings 19:9), letting him voice his fear, loneliness, and despair. The most important thing for an anxious person is empathy for those feelings, and God calmed him by co-regulating alongside him.
  • He gave him new strength — In a still, small voice he said, "Yet I will leave seven thousand in Israel" (1 Kings 19:18). Elijah thought he alone was left, but he was not alone.

David — Honest Lament Poured Out to God

David was called "a man after God's own heart" (Acts 13:22), yet reading his psalms shows how often he experienced anxiety and fear. In Psalm 13 he prays desperately, "How long, O LORD?" — four times over. In Psalm 22 he honestly expresses the feeling of abandonment: "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?" (Ps. 22:1) — the very verse Jesus quoted on the cross. Psalm 38 even describes physical symptoms like palpitations, fatigue, and dimming sight ("My heart throbs; my strength fails me," Ps. 38:10), and Psalm 55 holds the urge to escape: "Oh, that I had wings like a dove! I would fly away and be at rest" (Ps. 55:6).

Modern psychologists call David's psalms "an astonishingly accurate description of anxiety and depression." A 2017 study in a clinical-psychology journal found that the laments of the Psalms closely resemble the 'emotional expression' techniques of modern psychotherapy: when we pour out our suffering and questions to a safe object — God — without holding back, psychological pressure is released. Remarkably, almost all of David's laments end in praise ("But I have trusted in your steadfast love," Ps. 13:5). This movement 'from lament to praise' is a healthy way of moving toward hope without suppressing negative emotion. David's psalms give us freedom to hide nothing from God.

Paul — An Apostle Who Walked with a Thorn in the Flesh

Even the great apostle Paul had "a thorn in the flesh." "A thorn was given me in the flesh, a messenger of Satan to harass me" (2 Cor. 12:7). What it was isn't stated in Scripture, but Paul suffered greatly from it and pleaded with God three times to remove it, yet it was not healed. Many Christians who struggle with generalized anxiety disorder pray countless times for healing, but the anxiety doesn't completely disappear. It is not because their faith is lacking.

God answered, "My grace is sufficient for you, for my power is made perfect in weakness" (2 Cor. 12:9). Sometimes God does not remove our suffering but is with us within it. Paul accepted this answer, boasting in his weakness and learning to live with the thorn. He said, "We are afflicted in every way, but not crushed" (2 Cor. 4:8–9), and of his first arrival at Corinth, "I was with you in weakness and in fear and much trembling" (1 Cor. 2:3). Paul, too, was a person who felt fear and anxiety, yet through it all he depended on God and continued his ministry.

Jesus — Praying in Fear and Anguish

Jesus, fully God and fully man, also experienced fear and anguish. In Gethsemane, facing the cross, he was in extreme emotional pain: "My soul is very sorrowful, even to death" (Matt. 26:38). Luke records, "And his sweat became like great drops of blood falling down to the ground" (Luke 22:44) — a phenomenon known medically as hematidrosis, which, according to a 2018 report in the British Journal of Dermatology, can occur under extreme psychological pressure. Jesus prayed, "Let this cup pass from me; nevertheless, not as I will, but as you will" (Matt. 26:39). He chose to obey the will of God even while feeling fear. Hebrews says, "Although he was a son, he learned obedience through what he suffered" (Heb. 5:8). The Gethsemane prayer shows that to feel fear is not a sin, and that we can obey God even within fear.

Understanding the Command "Do Not Be Anxious" Correctly

"The Bible says 'do not be anxious,' but my worry won't stop. Am I being disobedient?" a young man asked. Misunderstanding this command can heap even greater guilt on those struggling with generalized anxiety disorder. The word translated 'be anxious' in Matthew 6:25 is the Greek merimnaō, meaning 'to be divided, to be torn in mind.' It is not simply 'to worry' but 'to be torn apart by obsessive worry.' What Jesus forbade is not anxiety itself but 'a life dominated by anxiety.' Responsibly planning for the future is different from being unable to live today because of compulsive worry.

The key is in what follows. Jesus did not only say "Do not worry"; he said what to do: "Seek first the kingdom of God and his righteousness" (Matt. 6:33). Stopping anxiety doesn't happen by mere resolve — it's a shift of focus: from our needs to the One who provides, from the problem to the One who can solve it, from the uncertainty of the future to the God who is present with us now. He also said, "Do not be anxious about tomorrow... Sufficient for the day is its own trouble" (Matt. 6:34). He didn't say there would be no trouble; he said there's no need to carry tomorrow's trouble today. To those with generalized anxiety disorder who carry the worries of next week, next month, and next year all today, Jesus says, "Focus on today. Today, God is with you."

You Weren't the Only One Anxious

Finishing her counseling, Sujin said, "I had no idea the people of the Bible were so afraid and trembled. It comforts me to know they were weak like me." Indeed — "Elijah was a man with a nature like ours" (James 5:17). It was not that they were special, but that the God who was with them was special.

Paul, in prison and not knowing when he would be executed, said, "Do not be anxious about anything, but in everything by prayer and supplication with thanksgiving let your requests be made known to God. And the peace of God, which surpasses all understanding, will guard your hearts and your minds in Christ Jesus" (Phil. 4:6–7). He too did not only say "Do not be anxious," but to pray, petition, and give thanks. A 2020 study in a journal of clinical psychology and religion found that these three elements align with the core principles of cognitive behavioral therapy: prayer is a Christian form of mindfulness, supplication aims at concrete problem-solving, and thanksgiving shifts perspective to quickly stabilize the nervous system. What is promised here is not "the problem will be solved" but "peace." The word for "guard," phroureō, is a military term meaning 'to keep watch, to protect' — the peace of God protects our hearts like a soldier guarding a fortress.

Peace comes not when circumstances change but when we experience God's presence within those circumstances. The God who gave Elijah food and rest, who listened to David's lament, who told Paul "My grace is sufficient," and who was with Jesus in Gethsemane is with you now. You are not alone.

I Feel Like God Is Going to Punish Me

Restoring a Distorted Image of God

"Pastor, honestly, I'm afraid of God." Jeongmin, in her mid-thirties, spoke with her head bowed. How we perceive God directly affects our anxiety level. The problem is that the image of God we hold is often distorted. We don't see God as revealed in Scripture, but through a lens of fear based on our experiences and wounds. This chapter explores four aspects of God's character that Scripture reveals.

The Sovereign God — Letting Go of the Illusion of Control

Jeongmin, a marketing professional, is a perfectionist. She schedules by the minute and falls apart when things go awry. Psychologists call this the 'illusion of control.' People with generalized anxiety disorder find uncertainty hard to bear and try to control it, but since life is fundamentally uncertain, the more they try to control, the greater the anxiety. Scripture offers a different perspective: "The LORD has established his throne in the heavens, and his kingdom rules over all" (Ps. 103:19). If God is not sovereign, the universe is chaos and we must protect ourselves; but if God is sovereign, nothing escapes his control, and our future is certain in him.

Junho, who failed in business and fell into great debt, read one sleepless night, "The heart of man plans his way, but the LORD establishes his steps" (Prov. 16:9), and his anxiety dropped greatly. Our lives are within God's providence ("all things work together for good," Rom. 8:28). Joseph, sold into slavery by his brothers, later confessed, "You meant evil against me, but God meant it for good" (Gen. 50:20). A 2019 study in a journal of psychology and religion reported that those who firmly believe in God's sovereignty had far lower anxiety in uncontrollable situations. But believing in sovereignty doesn't mean doing nothing — we must still live responsibly and plan wisely.

"I feel I'll be loved if I serve hard at church and become a 'good Christian.' Won't God be the same?" Jeongmin's words show the wound of 'conditional love' that many experience. A 2018 study in a developmental-psychology journal reported that conditional love in childhood significantly affects perfectionism, fear of failure, the incidence of generalized anxiety disorder, and low self-esteem. Under conditional love we must constantly prove 'I am worthy of love,' and no matter how hard we try it never seems enough — a thought that creates great anxiety.

But God does not love us conditionally. "God shows his love for us in that while we were still sinners, Christ died for us" (Rom. 5:8). "I have loved you with an everlasting love" (Jer. 31:3) — that love does not depend on our actions, and nothing can separate us from it (Rom. 8:39). Jesus spoke of this love in the parable of the prodigal son (Luke 15). To the younger son who returned after squandering his inheritance, the father ran from afar, embraced and kissed him, and before his confession was even finished gave him the best robe, a ring, and sandals, restoring him as a son. A 2017 study at Durham University in England analyzed this as active, initiating, unconditional love and the restoration of identity.

Eunkyung, who agonized for ten years over having had an abortion, suffered anxiety and depression from the thought, "God couldn't love me; I'm defiled by sin." But meditating on the parable of the prodigal son, she realized, "The father received him not as a servant but as a son — without conditions, immediately, completely," and she wept. "I believed that no matter how great my sin, God's love is greater. After that, much of my anxiety disappeared." 'Unconditional positive regard,' proposed by Carl Rogers, is an important element of modern psychotherapy. A 2020 meta-analysis in a counseling-psychology journal found that it raises self-esteem and reduces anxiety and depression. For someone who has experienced conditional love all their life, being accepted just as they are is transformative.

The God Who Keeps His Promises

"Promises can't be trusted. Not even God's promises." So said Jiwon, a graduate student who had endured her father's broken promises, a boyfriend who left, and friends who spread her secrets. From the perspective of attachment theory, a person with insecure attachment, raised in an untrustworthy environment, has higher anxiety. But the God of Scripture keeps his promises without fail. "God is not man, that he should lie" (Num. 23:19). History proves it: as he promised Abraham a great nation, Israel was born (Gen. 12:2); from David's line Jesus Christ came (2 Sam. 7:16); and as promised in the new covenant, the church was born (Jer. 31:33). The promises Scripture gives us are full provision (Phil. 4:19), presence (Matt. 28:20), and guidance (Ps. 32:8). When we hold on to these promises in prayer, faith arises and we enjoy peace.

Immanuel — The God Who Is with Us

"Even when I'm with people, I'm lonely. No one really knows me." So confessed Jiwon. Loneliness is one of the major causes of anxiety; those who experience chronic loneliness have a 2–3 times higher incidence of generalized anxiety disorder. When we're lonely, stress hormones like cortisol rise and the anxiety system activates. But we are never alone. In Scripture, "Do not be afraid" is followed by the reason: "for I am with you." One of Jesus' names, 'Immanuel,' means 'God is here with us now.' "I will never leave you nor forsake you" (Heb. 13:5).

Psalm 23 shows how God's presence drives out fear: "Even though I walk through the valley of the shadow of death, I will fear no evil, for you are with me" (Ps. 23:4). David does not deny the danger of the valley — it really is dangerous. Yet even within that danger he does not fear, because God is with him. Junhyeok, in his late thirties and diagnosed with cancer, confessed, "Reading Psalm 23 every day, I realized God walks this valley with me. The treatment was painful, but I was not alone." In a 2020 study in the Journal of Spirituality in Mental Health, participants who practiced 'the presence of God' — pausing several times a day to recognize 'God is here with me now' — for eight weeks saw their anxiety drop by an average of 31%.

  • When you wake up, pray, "God, thank you that you'll be with me all day today."
  • When anxiety rises, breathe deeply and confess, "God is here with me right now."
  • Set several alarms during the day, and at each one pause to recognize God's presence.
  • Before sleep, pray, "Thank you for being with me today. Be with me tomorrow too."

In this chapter we saw that knowing God — the Sovereign of our lives, who loves unconditionally, keeps his promises faithfully, and is always with us — heals anxiety. But knowing it in the head is not enough. We must actually experience this truth.

I Have Everything, but No Sense of Security

The True Peace Found in Jesus

Suhyeon graduated from a top university and is a lawyer at a large firm. He owns an apartment and a foreign car and travels abroad. "I've succeeded. I'm fairly satisfied. But my heart isn't at ease. The World's Peace and the Peace of Christ

According to a 2020 Harvard Business School study, the prevalence of anxiety disorders among high-income professionals was higher than in the general population, and even after achievement the relief lasted only briefly before they were seized by anxiety about the next goal. Success, wealth, and the attainment of goals cannot give peace. Reach one goal and a higher one appears; solve one problem and another arises. The world's peace, like a mirage, vanishes as you approach. The peace of Christ is different. It does not depend on circumstances, achievement, or external conditions. From prison, Paul wrote, "I have learned in whatever situation I am to be content... I have learned the secret of facing plenty and hunger, abundance and need" (Phil. 4:11–12).

The Peace Waiting for Me Beyond the Cross

The psychologist Ernest Becker, in his Pulitzer-winning The Denial of Death, argued that "at the root of all human anxiety is the fear of death." But the deeper reason we fear death is that beyond death there is judgment ("it is appointed for man to die once, and after that comes judgment," Heb. 9:27). Behind the fear of death lies sin ("the wages of sin is death," Rom. 6:23).

Misun, who suffered guilt for twenty years over an abortion in her teens, thought her sin was too great to be forgiven. But when her pastor asked, "Is the blood Jesus shed smaller than your sin?" she came to her senses, realizing she had regarded her sin as greater than Jesus' sacrifice. "Even now it saddens me to think of it. But the guilt is gone. I have been completely and eternally forgiven." This assurance removed the root of her anxiety.

If the cross resolved the problem of sin, the resurrection resolved the problem of death. "Christ has been raised from the dead, the firstfruits of those who have fallen asleep" (1 Cor. 15:20). If death is not the end, there is nothing to fear on this earth. Junsu, given a stage-4 cancer diagnosis, confessed at an Easter service, "I realized I'm not heading toward death but toward resurrection. Death is the door to my home." The cross confirms that our sins are forgiven and that we are worthy of love, and the resurrection gives hope of life beyond death. The gospel is not a ticket to heaven but the power that transforms our present life — and that power heals our anxiety too.

The Peace of an Entirely Different Identity

"I've lived all my life as the person others expected me to be, and I don't know who I really am." Confusion of identity is a major cause of anxiety. We often seek the ground of our identity outside ourselves — in performance ('I am what I do'; collapse if I fail), in possessions ('I am what I have'; fear of losing), in others' evaluation ('I am how others see me'; fear of rejection). When we entrust identity to externals, we are bound to be anxious. Scripture offers an entirely different identity.

  • A child of God — "he gave the right to become children of God" (John 1:12). It doesn't change with success or failure, and no one can take it away.
  • A royal priest — "you are a chosen race, a royal priesthood" (1 Pet. 2:9). Independent of social status or others' opinions.
  • The bride of Christ — "to present you as a pure virgin to Christ" (2 Cor. 11:2). Based not on how we are but on Christ's love.
  • A temple of the Spirit — "your body is a temple of the Holy Spirit within you" (1 Cor. 6:19). Based not on our holiness but on God's grace.

A deacon who experienced an identity crisis after being passed over for promotion confessed, "My identity was grounded in the wrong place. I should be defined not by my achievements but by who I am in Christ. Promoted or not, I am a child of God. That realization set me free." Closely tied to identity is security. The world gives conditional security (if you're strong, if you're lovable, if you succeed), but in Christ our security is unconditional. We are held doubly, in Jesus' hand and the Father's hand, so no one can snatch us away (John 10:27–29), and nothing can separate us from the love of Christ (Rom. 8:35). Minho, a missionary serving in a dangerous region, says, "I'm at peace because my safety doesn't depend on circumstances. I am in Jesus' hand."

The Peace of Walking with the Spirit

Jesus called the Holy Spirit the 'Helper' — the Greek paraklētos, meaning 'one called alongside, advocate, comforter, helper' (John 14:16–17). When we're anxious we sometimes don't know what to pray and only repeat "God..." That's all right. "The Spirit himself intercedes for us with groanings too deep for words" (Rom. 8:26). One fruit borne when the Spirit dwells in us is peace (Gal. 5:22; Rom. 8:6). So how do we experience the fullness of the Spirit?

  • Ask for the Spirit — "how much more will the heavenly Father give the Holy Spirit to those who ask him!" (Luke 11:13).
  • Obey — "the Holy Spirit, whom God has given to those who obey him" (Acts 5:32). Obedience is a channel of the Spirit.
  • Repent of sin — "If we confess our sins, he is faithful and just to forgive us... and to cleanse us" (1 John 1:9). Repentance makes room for the Spirit's work.
  • Fill your inner self with the Word — "Let the word of Christ dwell in you richly" (Col. 3:16).
  • Pray — "Pray without ceasing" (1 Thess. 5:17). Prayer is fellowship with the Spirit.

Jeonga prayed every morning, "Fill me with your Spirit today," and stayed conscious of the Spirit all day. When anxious she prayed, "Spirit, fill me with peace," and remarkably, she was at peace in situations that once would have dominated her with anxiety. "It's not a peace I made by effort. It's the peace the Spirit gives." In Old Testament times people had to go to the temple to meet God, but now we ourselves are the temple (1 Cor. 6:19). Paul urges us to "walk by the Spirit" (Gal. 5:16) — in the original, 'to walk in the Spirit,' a continuous process of being aware of the Spirit moment by moment and following his lead. In a 2019 study in the journal Pastoral Psychology, a group trained to recall hourly that 'God is within me' had markedly lower anxiety than a general group, because that awareness immediately reduced loneliness and isolation and lifted the burden of 'I have to handle this alone.'

In this chapter we examined the three dimensions of the peace found in Christ — peace through the cross and resurrection, peace from a new identity, and peace through the Spirit. The peace of Christ does not depend on circumstances, nor is it earned by our effort; it is a gift given right now. What truly heals anxiety is the gospel of the cross and the work of the Spirit, because the gospel addresses the roots of anxiety — guilt, identity, security, and the meaning of existence.

But God has given us various means of healing, and medical treatment is one of them.

Medications Used to Treat Anxiety

Generalized anxiety disorder is not merely a matter of mental control but is related to a biochemical imbalance in the brain. About 100 billion nerve cells communicate via neurotransmitters, and the key ones are serotonin (regulating mood, anxiety, sleep), GABA (calming excessive excitation), and norepinephrine (regulating arousal and stress response). According to a 2020 study by the American Psychiatric Association, abnormalities in the connection between the prefrontal cortex and the amygdala were found in about 70% of patients. This means the brain's ability to regulate anxiety is biologically diminished. So generalized anxiety disorder is not due to moral failure or a lack of faith. Just as a diabetic uses insulin, receiving medication is using the medical wisdom God has given.

  • Benzodiazepines — bind to GABA receptors to quickly (within an hour) calm the brain's excessive excitation, effective for acute anxiety. But because long-term use can cause dependence, tolerance, and withdrawal, they are generally used short-term and must follow a doctor's direction.
  • Buspirone — acts on a serotonin receptor (5-HT1A) to lower anxiety. It has little dependence and can be used long-term, but takes 2–4 weeks to take effect.
  • SSRIs (selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors) — the first-line treatment for generalized anxiety disorder. Non-addictive, safe long-term, and they also treat accompanying depression. Effects appear after 2–6 weeks, and early on there may be a temporary rise in anxiety, nausea, weight gain, etc.
  • SNRIs — inhibit reuptake of both serotonin and norepinephrine. Effective when SSRIs don't respond, or when chronic pain is also present.

The Process and Method of Medication

Treatment generally proceeds through initial evaluation (1–2 weeks) → adaptation (2–6 weeks, temporary side effects) → stabilization (6–12 weeks, maximum effect) → maintenance (6–12 months or more, greatly lowering relapse) → discontinuation (2–6 months, gradual tapering). Yeongsu, in his thirties, became dependent on a benzodiazepine (alprazolam), but by tapering slowly with his doctor while combining cognitive behavioral therapy, he successfully discontinued it after six months. Mikyung, in her fifties, who had struggled for over ten years, took an SSRI (escitalopram); for the first two weeks her anxiety actually increased and she nearly stopped, but hearing from her doctor that it was temporary she continued, improved from week four, and after eight weeks felt "normal for the first time in ten years." "When the fog of anxiety lifted, the words of Scripture sounded clearer and I could concentrate in prayer."

To maximize the effect, take it regularly (use an alarm), don't stop on your own even with side effects, take it with food if nauseated, in the evening if drowsy, in the morning if it causes insomnia. Exercising 3–4 times a week, sleeping well, and limiting caffeine and alcohol are also important. Studies at home and abroad all report that combining medication with cognitive behavioral therapy is more effective than either alone, with a lower relapse rate.

The Limits and Misconceptions of Medication

Medication has limits. About 30–40% don't respond to the first drug, requiring a different one or a combination; and medication eases neurobiological symptoms but doesn't change thought patterns or behavior itself, so psychotherapy is needed. But there are also many needless misconceptions. 'Once you take meds you can never stop' — many discontinue successfully after a few years, and where long-term treatment is needed it's like managing diabetes or hypertension. 'Medicine changes your personality' — rather, it removes the distorting lens of anxiety so you can return to your healthy, true self. 'Natural remedies are safer' — natural supplements are not standardized and risk interactions, whereas medications are approved after rigorous clinical trials.

Faith's View of Medication

Faith conflicts usually arise from mistaken theology. The idea that 'if you have real faith you don't need medicine' is a dualism that treats the spiritual as noble and the material as inferior. Scripture has many records of using medicinal substances like fig poultices, wine, and oil. The logic that 'medication means not depending on God' would make taking cold medicine, wearing glasses, or having surgery equally a failure to depend on God. Jesus affirmed the role of medicine: "Those who are well have no need of a physician, but those who are sick" (Matt. 9:12). The view that 'medicine only masks symptoms' also misses that, just as a cast on a broken leg does, medication creates the environment for healing.

The right view is this. First, God heals in various ways (the miracle in John 9, the natural recovery in Leviticus 13–14, Hezekiah's fig poultice in Isaiah 38:21). In a 2020 Fuller Seminary study, while most evangelical Christians believed 'God can heal through doctors and medicine,' only about 40% accepted it as God's healing when they themselves took psychiatric medication. Pastor Kim, who for thirty years taught "solve everything by faith," when he himself developed generalized anxiety disorder refused treatment out of concern for his image, until a severe panic attack sent him to the ER; afterward he confessed, "Rejecting the medical wisdom God gave me was pride." Second, our body is a temple of the Holy Spirit, so we must care for it with proper treatment (1 Cor. 6:19). Third, God works through process — just as he drove out the enemies "little by little" (Ex. 23:30) when Israel entered Canaan, healing too is a process that takes time. Historically, the church fathers Basil, Martin Luther, John Calvin, and C. S. Lewis all received medical help amid depression and anxiety. Lewis wrote in A Grief Observed, "It is not a disgrace to need medical help."

Medication Is a Grace from God

Medication is God's common grace. The scientists who developed anti-anxiety drugs discovered the natural laws God created. "The way of a fool is right in his own eyes, but a wise man listens to advice" (Prov. 12:15). Medication and prayer don't conflict. The right question is not 'Should I take medicine or pray?' but 'How shall I pray while taking medicine?' Hyeonju, who had struggled for ten years, experienced a dramatic change at week six of taking an antidepressant. "Anxiety was like a thick curtain blocking me from God. Now that curtain seems to have been drawn back. I can see that God was there all along."

The church community should accept medication as normal, not judge it as weak faith, and offer prayer and encouragement. Words like "It's because your faith is weak," "I heard you get addicted to meds," and "You'll be fine if you pray more" produce guilt and isolation. When Minsu, who began medication, was told in his small group "You should depend on God, not just on meds," he stopped attending. Had they responded, "Thank you for sharing courageously. You wisely started treatment. I'll pray for you," he wouldn't have had to hide himself at church.

I Don't Really Want to See a Doctor or Counselor

How to Find and Make Good Use of Professionals

  • Psychiatrist — check reputation, whether they specialize in anxiety disorders, explain fully, and respect the patient's opinion. They needn't be a Christian, but one who respects faith is good.

CHAPTER 09

I'll End Up Making Mistakes and Everything Will Go Wrong

Correcting Cognitive Distortions and Restructuring Thoughts

Mira, in her forties, begins each morning with the thought, 'What will go wrong today? I'll make mistakes and everything will fall apart.' She feels she isn't a good wife, a good mother, or a good Christian. Her counselor said, "You're anxious not because of the actual situation but because of your subjective interpretation of it," and proposed Christian cognitive behavioral therapy that changes our thoughts through the wisdom God gives in Scripture.

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy — Focusing on Thoughts

Cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT) began with Albert Ellis's pioneering work in the 1950s and was developed into a refined model by Aaron Beck in the 1960s. Its core principle is simple — our emotions and behaviors are determined not by the situation itself but by our interpretation of it. When a pastor suddenly says "Let's talk," Deacon A thinks 'What did I do wrong?' leading to anxiety, palpitations, and avoidance, while Deacon B thinks 'What encouragement does he have for me?' leading to curiosity, calm, and a relaxed meeting. The same situation, but different thoughts produce completely different experiences. "As a man thinks in his heart, so is he" (Prov. 23:7); "be transformed by the renewal of your mind" (Rom. 12:2) — here, renewing the 'mind' (nous) is the key to change. To do that, we must first notice what we are thinking.

Common Cognitive Distortions in Anxiety, and Scripture's Counsel

  • Catastrophizing — magnifying a small problem into the worst. An elder who misread one number at a finance meeting thought 'I may have to step down, and my business will suffer too' and lost sleep for three days, though most didn't even notice the mistake. → "Do not be anxious about tomorrow... Sufficient for the day is its own trouble" (Matt. 6:34).
  • All-or-nothing thinking — seeing things as 'all or nothing.' The perfectionist Mira, on a day she yelled, thought 'I'm a bad mother,' and after breaking her diet, 'It's all ruined,' falling into self-blame. → "The righteous falls seven times and rises again" (Prov. 24:16). Peter too denied Jesus three times but was restored to be the rock.
  • Mind reading — concluding others' thoughts without evidence. Gayoung, thinking 'Everyone looked at me strangely,' checked social media and avoided gatherings. → "Man looks on the outward appearance, but the LORD looks on the heart" (1 Sam. 16:7). When you actually ask, often no one thinks that.
  • Overgeneralization — generalizing one or two experiences as 'always/never.' Jeongwoo fell into helplessness thinking 'God never hears my prayers; I'm a failure of a life.' → "His mercies... are new every morning" (Lam. 3:22–23).
  • Emotional reasoning — accepting a momentary feeling as fact without reality-testing. Mikyung thought, 'I feel anxious, so something dangerous will happen; God feels distant, so he has left me.' Aaron Beck called such 'automatic thoughts' that aren't helpful 'dysfunctional automatic thoughts.' → "The heart is deceitful above all things" (Jer. 17:9); hold on to the unchanging promise, "I will never leave you" (Heb. 13:5).
  • Should statements — rigid thinking that binds self and others with '~must.' Younghee drove herself with 'I must not look weak, a Christian must not get angry, I must not miss dawn prayer,' feeling guilt at the smallest failure. → "My grace is sufficient for you" (2 Cor. 12:9). Our standard is not perfection but the grace Jesus gives.

Rebuilding Thoughts with the Truth of Scripture

Based on Romans 12:2, we can restructure cognitive distortions in three steps — recognizing false thoughts ("do not be conformed to this world") → replacing them with truth ("renewal of your mind") → a changed life (discerning God's will). After his diagnosis, Minjun, who thought 'God must be punishing me,' meditated on "no condemnation" (Rom. 8:1), "that the works of God might be displayed in him" (John 9:3), and "all things work together for good" (Rom. 8:28), and revised his thought to 'God isn't punishing me; he awakens me in love.' Six months later he started a support group for others with the same struggle, confessing, "I see now this was not punishment but meaningful suffering."

Practical Tools — The Truth Card and the Thought Record

  • Truth card — On the front, a distorted thought ("I'm a failure"); on the back, Scripture ("The righteous falls seven times and rises again," Prov. 24:16) and a restructured thought ("Failure isn't my identity... God wants obedience and growth"). Minjun made ten cards and read them whenever anxious; eventually the truth came up automatically without the cards.
  • Thought Record — the core tool of CBT. Record the date, situation, automatic thought, emotion (0–100), physical reaction, evidence search, alternative thought, and re-rated emotion. When no one replied in a group chat, Mira wrote 'I'm not welcome' (anxiety 85), then with the alternative thought 'It's a weekday morning and everyone's busy; people's reactions don't determine my worth; my worth is in Jesus,' she lowered her anxiety to 30.

Over three weeks of keeping a thought record, Mira found that her anxiety peaked not when there was real danger but when it involved others' evaluation, and that 90% of her automatic thoughts never came true. Keeping the record halved her anxiety. When we notice thoughts that flow by unconsciously, pause, and evaluate them against the truth, we can leave falsehood and abide in truth.

I Want to Get Rid of Anxiety Completely

In quicksand, flailing sinks you deeper, but staying still opens space to get out.

Acceptance and Commitment Therapy — Walking As You Are

Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT) was developed by Steven Hayes in the 1980s. Rather than just removing symptoms, it builds psychological flexibility so you can live a valued life. If CBT changes negative thoughts, ACT accepts negative thoughts and feelings as they are while still acting according to core values. Its six elements remarkably align with the teachings of Scripture.

  • Acceptance — not suppressing painful feelings but acknowledging, 'There's anxiety right now.' Just as "don't think of a white bear" makes it pop up more, 'I mustn't be anxious' makes you more anxious. Jeonghui, who fought anxiety, treated it like a guest. "Anxiety has come. Hi, anxiety. It's okay that you're here; I'll do what I need to do." After a month her anxiety eased, and above all, not fighting kept her from losing energy. → "Count it all joy... when you meet trials" (James 1:2–3), "give thanks in all circumstances" (1 Thess. 5:18).
  • Cognitive defusion — separating thoughts from self, seeing a thought as merely a 'thought.' Labeling — "There's that 'I'll mess up' thought again" (Cheolsu) — or writing it on paper and setting it on the desk, or singing it to a silly tune, weakens its power. → "Take every thought captive" (2 Cor. 10:5). We fight not the thoughts themselves but the thoughts that deceive us.
  • Present-moment contact — placing awareness on the here and now rather than past regret or future worry. When anxious, the five-senses exercise (5 things to see → 4 to hear → 3 to feel → 2 to smell → 1 to taste) helps. Deacon Jeong, whose mind filled with distractions during worship, after three weeks of training to focus on breath, the feeling of feet, and the Word, heard the sermon for the first time. → "Be still, and know that I am God" (Ps. 46:10). The Hebrew for 'be still,' raphah, means 'to let go.'
  • Self as context — saying "I'm experiencing anxiety right now" instead of "I'm an anxious person." Then you move from 'anxiety itself' to 'the self that notices anxiety,' gaining room to act toward what you value without being swept away. A Christian, in any situation, doesn't forget the identity of being God's child who walks with the Spirit.
  • Values — clarifying, as a verb, what truly matters to you. Elder Choi, who gave up missions, service, and challenges because of anxiety, when asked "What would you do if you weren't anxious?" answered through tears, "I'd preach the gospel on the mission field, mentor young adults, deepen my relationship with my wife, and use my gifts." The counselor asked, "Will you wait until anxiety is gone, or will you live by your precious values even with anxiety?"
  • Committed action — practicing concrete actions in line with values. Elder Choi started small with one-on-one youth mentoring, a short-term mission trip, and intimate conversations with his wife. "I can do meaningful things even with anxiety. In fact, doing what matters made anxiety less important." → "Seek first the kingdom of God" (Matt. 6:33), "faith apart from works is dead" (James 2:26).

You Can Live On Even While Anxious

The lesson of ACT is clear: don't fight to eliminate anxiety, but accept it and move toward your values. Even with anxiety, you can love, work, worship, and dream. Philippians 4:6–7 doesn't promise anxiety will instantly vanish; it says "the peace of God will guard your hearts and your minds." Peace is not the absence of anxiety but the state of being with God. Because God is with us, we can live on even while anxious.

I'm Not in the Mood to Do Anything

Managing Mood Through Small Actions

Action Moves Emotion

Depression and anxiety lead to avoidance, and avoidance narrows the range of activity, which adds more depression and anxiety — a vicious cycle of 'anxiety → avoidance → isolation → greater anxiety.' By contrast, acting first creates a virtuous cycle of 'action → accomplishment → confidence → reduced anxiety.' This is called 'Behavioral Activation' (BA). A 1996 study by psychologist Neil Jacobson and others proved that focusing on behavior alone is as effective as, or more effective than, cognitive therapy. Mood is the result of action, not its precondition. Action activates the brain's reward circuit to release dopamine (even small acts like making the bed) and gives a sense of control. Biblically, it is obeying God's Word in action. "If you obey the voice of the LORD your God... all these blessings shall come upon you" (Deut. 28:2). Moses obeyed and crossed the Red Sea; Naaman set aside his pride, obeyed, and was healed of leprosy.

Starting with Something Very Small

The pastor urged Minsu to start with "something so small you can't fail." Walk to the bathroom → wash your face → walk to the living room → look out the window for one minute. "Once you start moving, you want to keep moving — like the law of inertia." Holding on to "One who is faithful in a very little is also faithful in much" (Luke 16:10), begin with small obedience. The concrete practice has three stages.

  • Stage 1: Plan and record activities — For a week, write down time, activity, mood (0–10), anxiety (0–10), and accomplishment (0–10) to visualize the link between mood and behavior. It reveals which activities drag you down (avoidance) and which give energy (activation).
  • Stage 2: Find meaningful activities — Dopamine works more for motivation and seeking than mere pleasure, so the effect grows when an action has meaning (value-based action). Make an activity list across the areas of spiritual growth, relationships, health, creativity, service, and learning.
  • Stage 3: Increase activities little by little — Break a goal into steps of under five minutes ('put on workout clothes → put on shoes → open the front door → ...') and check off each ('staircase of obedience'). Increase week by week, like QT → QT + walk → + small-group meeting → + conversation with family.

Minsu, who started by laying out his clothes Saturday night, then just washing in the morning, then just dressing, then going only as far as the front door, ended up reaching church and worshiping for the first time in a month. "If I'd waited until I felt better, I'd have done nothing. Once I started, my mood improved. I felt that God was pleased with my small obedience."

Trying Exposure Therapy

'Exposure Therapy' repeatedly faces a feared object in a safe environment to learn it isn't threatening. On the principle that avoiding a fear enlarges it while facing it shrinks it, it produces extinction learning, habituation, and self-efficacy. Seonghun, who had social anxiety, made a hierarchy of feared situations (walking alone 15 → asking at a store 35 → ordering at a café 45 → ... → presenting before 100 people 100) and exposed himself gradually from the lowest. In week one, asking "Where are the eggs?" at a store, his anxiety rose to 60 then dropped to 20 after ten minutes; after eight weeks he was speaking up at small-group meetings. "Repeating it really did reduce the anxiety!"

Scripture also holds the principle of exposure. Marching around Jericho daily (Joshua 6) was exposure that grew very anxious at first then 'habituated,' and the sending of the seventy (Luke 10) corresponds to in-vivo exposure therapy. Jesus did not hide the danger ("I am sending you out as lambs in the midst of wolves," Luke 10:3 — realistic expectation-setting), repeated "Do not be afraid" (an anxiety-reduction intervention), and gave authoritative assurance. The joy of the returning seventy ("even the demons are subject to us in your name!" Luke 10:17) shows the surge in self-efficacy after a mastery experience. Albert Bandura emphasized that the most powerful way to reduce anxiety is a direct 'mastery experience,' which Jesus applied to disciple training 2,000 years ago. Where the world's exposure therapy rests on the human assurance "you can endure this," Christian exposure is built on Immanuel's promise: "I am with you always, to the end of the age" (Matt. 28:20).

There is also 'interoceptive exposure' for those who fear bodily sensations themselves — safely inducing dizziness by hyperventilating, palpitations by jogging in place, or breathlessness by breathing through a straw, to learn 'this sensation isn't dangerous.' Deaconess Lee Jiyeon, who panicked at palpitations ('I'm having a heart attack!'), deliberately jogged for two minutes to induce them, but no heart attack came; after four weeks of repetition she could think, "This is just adrenaline; it's not dangerous." The spiritual meaning of exposure is wilderness training. Israel's forty years in the wilderness were uncomfortable (exposure), drove them to depend on God (coping skills), and strengthened their faith (self-efficacy). CHAPTER 12

Three Spiritual Practices for Casting Your Cares

Growing Close to God Through Prayer, the Word, and Praise

"I've gone to church for over twenty years, read the Bible a lot, and prayed hard. So why am I so anxious? Is there something wrong with my faith?" For Eunmi, a homemaker in her fifties, anxiety triggered by her husband's business failure had grown until she had palpitations and trembling hands. According to a 2018 Baylor University study, about 23% of Christians who attend worship regularly suffer from an anxiety disorder, and many report that faith provides no practical help in handling anxiety. This chapter explores concrete, proven ways to apply three spiritual practices—prayer, Scripture meditation and memorization, and praise—to managing anxiety.

Anxiety-Reinforcing Prayer vs. Prayer That Casts Cares

We know "Cast all your anxieties on him, because he cares for you" (1 Pet. 5:7), but many don't know 'how' to cast them. Eunmi prayed "God, help me" every night, yet was still anxious; examining her prayer, she was actually just listing worries — 'God, what about the loan? What if I can't pay tuition?' Dr. Harold Koenig's team at Duke University called this 'Anxiety-Reinforcing Prayer.' Replaying the problem reinforces anxiety, because the brain can't distinguish a vivid imagining from a real event, so the amygdala reacts as if there's real danger each time she pictures future disaster. The pastor taught her a 'Surrender Prayer.'

  • Step 1: Name your worries concretely — "God, there are three worries on my heart: the loan interest, my children's tuition, and my husband's new business plan."
  • Step 2: Hand each worry over in a tangible way — write the list on paper and do a symbolic act of giving it to God, like folding it into a prayer box or between the pages of your Bible.
  • Step 3: Declare your surrender — "I now hand these worries over to you, Father. I will not hold on to them anymore."
  • Step 4: Turn to thanksgiving — "Thank you for already taking this on; thank you for feeding and clothing us until now."
  • Step 5: Respond when worry returns — "I've already given this to you. I won't take it back. Give me courage and strength to bear it."

According to a University of Pennsylvania study, a group that practiced this 'Surrender Prayer' for six weeks had significantly lower anxiety than a general-prayer group; the 'physical act of surrender'—writing worries down or putting them in a box—was especially effective at calming the amygdala's over-activation. Three months later Eunmi testified, "The worry hasn't completely gone, but it no longer crushes me as before. The problem is still there, but I'm sure now that it's no longer 'mine' but 'God's.'"

From Mere Reading to Meditation That Ruminates

Junho read three chapters daily, aiming to read the Bible through in a year, but when he heard of restructuring at work, his mind went blank and not a single verse came to him. "I realized my Bible reading had been close to collecting knowledge and information." The pastor taught him a meditation method (QT) adapted for anxiety: preparatory prayer → reading slowly aloud → dwelling on a single word or verse → applying it concretely → turning the Word into prayer → experiencing God's presence in silence. The word "anything" in Philippians 4:6 struck Junho. "Anything... really not be anxious about anything? The restructuring too? The loan too?"

Managing Anxiety Through Scripture Memorization

Remarkably, even people with no faith grasped those words in that moment like a lifeline. According to research by Dr. Herbert Benson of Harvard Medical School, repeatedly reciting Scripture triggers the 'Relaxation Response,' lowering heart rate, blood pressure, and anxiety hormones. Junho received five core verses (Phil. 4:6–7 for general anxiety, Isa. 41:10 for fear, Ps. 23:4 for crisis, Matt. 6:34 for worry about the future, 1 Pet. 5:7 for all cares) and repeated them three times a day. Three weeks later, when he heard more bad news, this time Isaiah 41:10 came to mind automatically and his heart was calmed. This is what 'engraving truth on the heart' looks like.

Moving into Praise with the Psalms

"I feel left out even during worship. When I see people raising their hands and weeping in praise, I'm envious—everyone else is close to God, and only I feel like an outsider." So said Hyeonju, in her late twenties. The pastor told her, "Praise isn't something you can only do when you feel good. We choose to praise. Even when we can't sense or feel it, God is God, so we praise." Reading Psalm 42 aloud, Hyeonju wept, "That's exactly how I feel," and saw that the psalmist, even in despair, told his own soul to "hope in God and praise him."

Brain-science research shows praise activates the prefrontal cortex, improving emotional regulation and lowering the stress hormone cortisol. According to a 2018 Harvard School of Public Health study, those with regular worship and spiritual habits had about a 33% lower risk of depression. Above all, praise shifts focus from 'me and my problems' to 'God.' Praise tames anxiety through breath control (singing induces abdominal breathing → activates the parasympathetic nervous system), cognitive restructuring (from 'I'm weak' to 'the Lord keeps me'), a channel for emotional expression (the Psalms even bring fear, sorrow, and anger to God), and spiritual warfare (the presence of God "enthroned on the praises" (Ps. 22:3) drives out fear). Sujin, who suffered panic disorder and couldn't ride the subway, made a praise playlist and sang along during an attack; six months later she said, "Within five minutes praise calms me. Praise has become my medicine."

Even after trying all of this, you may still be anxious. The most important thing is the truth that even my state—still anxious, not yet able to set down every worry—is fully known to God, who is with me. I haven't laid down all my worries yet, but he waits beside me, ready to bear them all. Whenever anxiety rises, I simply hand it back to God again. These three practices are not techniques for eliminating anxiety but a process of growing more intimate with God, and in that process anxiety naturally recedes.

1 Peter 5:7 — Cast all your anxieties on him, because he cares for you. (ESV)

CHAPTER 13

Setting Down All Effort and Abiding Before God

Spiritual Mindfulness That Sustains Life

"Pastor, when I close my eyes to pray and stay still, I feel suffocated. I'm more at ease keeping busy." Deacon Park, in his mid-fifties, used to pray fervently in the front pew, but anxiety beginning a few years ago made even prayer hard: the moment he said "God," worries about his children and finances surfaced and his chest tightened. Prayer became another source of stress. The pastor gently counseled, "Sometimes simply abiding quietly before God, without a word, is deep prayer."

From Mindfulness to Spiritual Mindfulness

Mindfulness is non-judgmental awareness of one's inner state and surroundings, reducing automatic, destructive thoughts. In clinical practice it serves as the core mechanism of MBSR, MBCT, ACT, and dialectical behavior therapy (DBT), and is especially effective for anxiety disorders' over-activation of thought, automatization of emotional reactions, and chronic physical tension. Some Christians mistake meditation for the exclusive domain of Buddhism or the New Age, but the Latin root of 'meditation,' meditatio, was an important spiritual practice in the medieval Christian tradition—meditating on Scripture and engraving God's Word on the heart. What matters is not the term but the direction.

Christian meditation differs fundamentally from other religions'. Where other religions center on the self, emptiness, or cosmic energy and aim at enlightenment or self-transcendence, Christian meditation has the triune God as its central object and aims at personal fellowship with God and sanctification. Because of the term's connotations, this book calls Christian meditation 'spiritual mindfulness'—'a spiritual practice of attentively recognizing God's presence through the indwelling of the Spirit, opening one's whole being to the Spirit to abide in God's love, and responding with spiritual discernment and surrender.' Its features: ① attention extends from oneself to God, ② it is not mere intellectual understanding but an experience of being, and ③ it is not gaining something through practice but dwelling in the presence of the God who already indwells us.

Spiritual Mindfulness in Practice — Deacon Park's Change

Deacon Park had prayed aloud, but the moment he tried to pray his anxiety spiked, so he avoided prayer altogether. 'I'm told to pray without ceasing, but I can't even pray—has my relationship with God been cut off?'—this self-blame amplified his anxiety. In the spiritual-mindfulness training the pastor proposed, Deacon Park did not try to remove the anxiety but practiced staying in that place while feeling it. Over time the anxiety didn't vanish completely, but it swept him away less, and above all the sense of being with God within the anxiety returned. "Now I feel it's okay even if I can't pray well. I've come to know that simply being with God is itself prayer."

Practicing Spiritual Mindfulness — Three Flows

Spiritual mindfulness is not a mere breathing technique but an integrative process of reorienting attention and centering one's being on God. It follows three flows.

  • Focusing attention — Gathering the scattered mind and turning attention toward abiding before God. Feel your breath and bodily sensations, but go one step further to be aware that this present moment is a place of being 'before Immanuel, here and now.'
  • Prayerful awareness — Not many words but an attitude of listening. Without suppressing or being swept by anxious thoughts, notice 'there's anxiety in me now' while not missing 'God is with me even in this moment.' When this dual awareness happens together, the intensity of emotion eases.
  • Compassion and mercy — As we repeatedly receive ourselves through God's eyes, self-condemnation gives way to self-compassion, which extends to compassion for others and the world.

When anxiety is severe, what's needed is not greater effort but a change of direction—from speaking prayer to abiding prayer, from controlling to entrusting. Sit quietly, feel your breath, and even when thoughts arise, don't try to erase them; just repeat a very short phrase: "Lord, I am here. Lord, you are with me." As Deacon Park continued, he came to feel God not as 'one I must go and find' but as 'one who has already been walking with me and is here now,' and his relationship with God shifted from 'doing' to 'being.' A secure attachment formed—knowing he wouldn't be abandoned even when anxious. "Jesus said, 'I am the vine; you are the branches' (John 15:5). The branch's job isn't to produce fruit but to stay attached to the vine. Just stay attached to the Lord. That's enough."

Psalm 46:10 — Be still, and know that I am God. (ESV)

CHAPTER 14

The Joy I Finally Came to Enjoy

Training to Grow and Enjoy the Capacity for Joy

Many Christians live without knowing how to enjoy joy. While we strain to reduce anxiety, we've hardly ever trained to grow joy. This chapter looks at the life of joy and gratitude proposed by Relational Skills Training (Thrive), which integrates interpersonal neurobiology and spirituality.

As Relational Beings, We Are Restored in Relationship

Interpersonal neurobiology (IPNB, systematized by psychiatrist Daniel Siegel) explains that our brain and mind are formed in, and restored through, relationship. Curt Thompson reinterprets this through faith, explaining that our brain heals through the experience of being 'known and loved.' The human brain is designed from birth to connect, and the very structure of the brain changes depending on the relationships we experience. In safe, warm relationships we feel secure, but in wounding relationships anxiety increases. Anxiety is not from weak willpower but a reaction when the threat-detecting brain circuit is over-activated. So healing always happens in relationship. The word "It is not good that the man should be alone" (Gen. 2:18) carries the operating principle of our brain and the order of creation. Sujeong, who thought handling problems alone was the grown-up thing, felt relieved to learn she often feels anxious because her brain's threat-detecting region reacts sensitively, and took hope that her neural network could change through new relational experiences.

The Rhythm of Joy and Rest; Growing the Capacity for Joy

A healthy person moves freely between two states—'joy' (heightened vitality) and 'rest' (relaxed calm)—maintaining emotional balance. When this rhythm is steady, the brain's emotional-regulation capacity grows, so that even amid suffering one can remain oneself. Joy is a powerful antidote to anxiety; a person with a large capacity for joy isn't easily overwhelmed by life's waves. Eunho, an executive at a securities firm, produced results but couldn't control his rising anger and threw harsh words at his family; only after a diagnosis of angina did he agree to the Relational Skills Training his wife had long urged. The first exercise is simple—sit knee-to-knee with a partner, silently convey "I'm glad to be with you" with your eyes, rest briefly when the joy peaks, and repeat, sharing how your body felt.

A high-energy emotion like anger needs a cooling device. An already-heated amygdala can be cooled by the 'rest' coolant. A well-trained brain has a developed prefrontal cortex that stabilizes the over-activated amygdala. Eunho, lacking this regulating function, vented anger; momentarily refreshing, anger's cortisol and adrenaline strain the cardiovascular system long-term, the prefrontal cortex is temporarily paralyzed, and relationships are destroyed. "Rejoice always" (1 Thess. 5:16) means keeping the state of joy even in anxiety-provoking situations, which comes from training in joy and rest. True joy begins in relational connection.

Gratitude Calms the Nervous System

An anxious mind focuses on what's lacking; gratitude is the opposite—an attitude of discovering and acknowledging the good already in one's situation. "My cup overflows" (Ps. 23:5). Gratitude has two parts—knowing that what you have is good, and recognizing that it was given from outside (for Christians, from God) rather than earned by your own strength. So Christians are people equipped to be grateful. Professor Michael McCullough of UC San Diego says, "The moment you pause to consider the gratitude given to you, your emotion is already moving from fear to a very good state." Expressing gratitude releases oxytocin, serotonin, and dopamine; oxytocin in particular creates the joy and security that come from relationship, so the deeper the gratitude, the larger the capacity for joy and the smaller the space anxiety occupies. "My wife brought me here, but now I'm telling her thank you. I never thought such gratitude would come out of my mouth," Eunho said. Even those for whom gratitude is unfamiliar can train it, starting small. "Give thanks in all circumstances" (1 Thess. 5:18)—today's warm cup of coffee, a child's smile, a line of praise that moved your heart are all God's gifts.

  • Three thanks a day — Before sleep, write three things you're grateful for and feel the joy and peace they bring.
  • Express gratitude in relationship — Sincerely tell a loved one your thanks for their presence, character, or actions, in person, by phone, or by text.
  • Share thanks to God — Tell a partner three aspects of God's character you're grateful for, with concrete examples, and share together.

CHAPTER 15

Talking Within a Safe Relationship

Expressing Pain and Forming an Attachment with God

Minjun, a seminary student, was known as faithful and kind; he helped people in hard situations and empathized well with others' pain. Yet when he himself was anxious, he couldn't express it—not to family, friends, or even God—because since childhood he had never seen anyone voice their feelings. Matthew D. Lieberman, a UCLA neuroscientist, says, "Affect-labeling—naming and describing emotions—can by itself regulate emotion and reduce the pain of negative feeling," because when we notice an emotion and put it into words, the activated limbic response connects with the prefrontal cortex, enabling regulation. Curt Thompson likewise sees that change begins only when a person tells the story of their pain before an empathizing other.

The Change That Comes When Anxiety Is Put Into Words

Memory works in two ways—'implicit memory' experienced as bodily sensation and emotion but hard to verbalize, and 'explicit memory' organized as story and language. Anxiety is often first experienced at the implicit level, so an unnamed feeling remains a fragmentary memory, repeatedly reactivated by small cues. But when these fragments are woven into a 'story' within a safe relationship, the brain finally integrates and finds peace. Many prayers in the Psalms are such stories—pouring out feelings honestly before God. "When I remember God, I moan; when I meditate, my spirit faints" (Ps. 77:3). After verbalizing his anxiety before God, the psalmist moves to remembering God's greatness.

In Relational Skills Training one practices: ① describe a simple situation, ② write briefly the thought it triggered, ③ list the emotions in detail, ④ describe what your body felt, ⑤ write where you acted like your true, Christ-like self (or how you wish you'd acted), and ⑥ share it with a partner, making eye contact and conveying genuine emotion. Writing out being corrected by a professor, Minjun realized that the vague unpleasant feeling was shame, and that he took the professor's advice as scolding because of childhood memories of being scolded by his father. When he shared the story, his partner, Deacon Kim, listened with empathy and embraced him, and Minjun felt his heart settle peacefully.

Meeting at Last the Secure Base, God

"I long thought of God as someone keeping score—pass if I do well, fail if I don't. But now God's gaze toward me feels different. Not strict, but warm." So said Eunji, a university student. A major cause of anxiety is the dread of broken connection and abandonment. Because we are designed to connect, the brain sends danger signals when connection is threatened, so the most important way to handle anxiety is to experience safe connection—what attachment theory calls a 'secure base.' God can be our ultimate secure base. Curt Thompson stresses that even those who lacked secure attachment in childhood can form a new attachment experience ('earned secure attachment') within a relationship with God. When "Fear not, for I am with you" (Isa. 41:10) becomes not mere knowledge but real experience engraved on the body, it becomes power to regulate anxiety.

Fellowship with God Through Immanuel Journaling

'Immanuel Journaling,' developed by Jim Wilder and others on the basis of Karl Lehmann's Immanuel approach, is a two-way dialogue practice of recognizing God's presence, listening to what he says, and writing it down. It is effective for managing anxiety because, through 'the concrete experience of being loved in the present moment,' an attachment with God forms; by the principle of neuroplasticity, recording the dialogue builds new neural networks where connection and security take root in place of anxiety.

  • Step 1: Interact with gratitude — Tell God your thanks, then write how God, delighting in your gratitude, would respond.
  • Step 2: Align your thoughts with God — From God's perspective, write his five hearts: "I can see you," "I can hear you," "I can understand how hard this is for you," "I am glad to be with you, and I have compassion on your weakness," and "I can do something about what you're going through."
  • Step 3: Read the journal aloud — Share it with others, where a group identity as God's children forms.

Eunji, anxious and lonely over job-hunting, wrote, following the form: "Beloved daughter, I see you at your desk, checking job postings with trembling hands... It's not that you must achieve something to receive my love. I hold your weakness just as it is, and I will never leave you alone... I am the good shepherd of your life. I've already prepared the way you'll go, so do not be anxious." As she wrote, her anxiety settled, and assured of being loved, she wept and gained courage to try again.

Healing is not the absence of pain but experiencing that, in the very midst of pain, Immanuel God is with us. Healing's aim is not to remove the problem but to meet God through it. Telling our feelings within a safe community and forming a secure attachment with God through Immanuel Journaling make relational healing possible. Jesus promised, "I will not leave you as orphans" (John 14:18).

Isaiah 41:10 — Fear not, for I am with you; be not dismayed, for I am your God. (ESV)
Epilogue — Even in Anxiety, Love Is Holding You

Even in Anxiety, Love Is Holding You

Do you remember the moment you first opened this book? Your hand may have trembled a little, turning the first page with a mix of doubt—'Will this really help me?'—and small courage—'Let me try anyway.' And now you've come this far. To wrestle with anxiety yet not give up, to keep seeking help, takes far more courage than the world imagines.

On the Illusion of Complete Recovery

Having read this book doesn't make anxiety vanish like magic. It will still visit you. But that is not failure—it is the process of recovery. We still live in a broken world, in frail bodies. Even the apostle Paul lived with a thorn in the flesh; though he pleaded three times for its removal, God said, "My grace is sufficient for you" (2 Cor. 12:9). Your anxiety, too, may not completely disappear. But what matters is that it need no longer rule you. With anxiety you can still love, work, worship, and dream as you move forward. This is the one message this book has tried to deliver to the end—a life lived well with God, even while anxious.

How to Celebrate Small Victories

On the road of recovery we easily miss the eye that recognizes small victories. Did you get out of bed today? That's a victory. Did you wash your face? Also a victory. Did you send a short text to a friend, walk outside even ten minutes, or sit in a worship seat carrying a heavy heart? All of these are victories. We tend to think only complete recovery counts as success, but if you're a little better than yesterday, that's a victory; a little less anxious than last week, that's a victory. Those small victories pile up to change the whole of who you are.

You Are Not Alone

On this journey you are never alone. "The LORD your God is the one who goes with you. He will not leave you or forsake you" (Deut. 31:6). Professionals too—the psychiatrist, the counselor, the pastor—are gifts God has prepared for you. Be with a 'hesed' community that responds to weakness with love. Don't hold out alone; true community begins when we reveal our weakness. And countless others walking the same road are with you—some ahead, some alongside, some behind.

Today, Just One Step

Don't try to change everything at once. Choose just one thing today—keep a thought record, take a walk, write down three things you're grateful for, or muster the courage to book a counseling appointment. One is enough. Tomorrow choose another, moving one step at a time. Some days you may stall. That's okay. What matters is direction. When you fall, get up; when you're tired, rest a while—but don't give up. Paul confessed, "Forgetting what lies behind and straining forward to what lies ahead, I press on toward the goal" (Phil. 3:13–14). He was not a perfect man, yet he kept running.

May the day come when you reach out to someone walking the same road and say, "I went through it too. It was so hard. But I'm okay now. You will surely be okay too." That day will surely come, because God is faithful.

Numbers 6:24–26 — The LORD bless you and keep you... and give you peace. Amen. (ESV)
